A SORT OF FRANKENSTEIN

by Jeremy Radtke

Project #2: A machine that looks for inspiration from 18th century automatons (again), Mary Shelly, H.P. Lovecraft, and my fourth grade science teacher Mrs. Snabon.

From an 8th century muslim alchemist attempts to add life to snakes making them subject to their creator's control, to an 18th century frenchman's mechanical duck with a functioning digestive system reanimation of dead things has been interesting problem to solve since the dawn of invention. In literature, art, medicine and philosophy, escaping death has been a point of intrest. Permit me to give the cliched examples of Mary Shelley, H.P. Lovecraft, Rene Descarte, George Romero, and Arne Larsson (the first person to receive an implantable pacemaker).

As an artist, I too have tried to make attempt at reanimation. Some years ago, I made a kinetic machine that moved a set of chicken wings. The wings, bought at a small bodega in South Providence, were attached to a mechanical device modeled after early flying machines. The device was complex system of gears and pulleys and was powered by an industrial farm motor. The wings were allowed to flap like they did in life. The photos below show the machine after the wings were removed due to rot. An attempt at taxidermy was tried and failed.

For my semester long project, I want to revisit the idea of reanimation. This time by manipulating the life cycle of a butterfly, something that we all are familiar with from grade school.

the life cycle of a buterfly:

My idea is adding another step onto this particular creature's cycle. After death, an individual butterfly is scanned into the computer and a laser cut mount is made (please see top image). Each mount is hinge in the center to allow for flexibility between the two different butterfly wings. Fifty different butterflies will be mounted and placed on cams, solenoids, and sensors. Belt driven cams or solenoids placed above the suspended butterflies will pull at the center joint, giving the creatures the power of "flight". The fifty butterflies will be divided into three different machines or squadrons. Each squadron will run on a track and will have limited movement around the space. When a viewer enters the space where the different squadrons are hung, each machine will react to where the viewer is in the room. If the viewer approaches the grouping of butterflies, the whole mechanism will move on its track to avoid contacting the viewer.

A drawing will follow.
